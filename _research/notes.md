## Subject area 1: inmate-tablets

   - Authors: Mack Finkel and Wanda Bertram. Updated 2024.

   - URL: https://www.prisonpolicy.org/blog/2017/07/06/tablets/
   - Desk research; families; foundational

3. **Open Campus / Slate survey (2023)** – "How People in Prison Really Use Technology." Survey of 80+ incarcerated people on tablet use. Slate, Sept 2023. 
   - URL: https://slate.com/technology/2023/09/prison-tech-survey-viapath-securus.html
   - Survey; incarcerated; foundational
   - NOTE: This is journalism from Slate / Open Campus. The methodology is described as "surveyed more than 80 people in prison about their experiences with tablets." Credible journalism org.

4. **Prison Legal News (2025)** – "Pay-for-Play Tablets: The Costly New Prison Paradigm." March 2025.
   - URL: https://www.prisonlegalnews.org/news/2025/mar/1/pay-play-tablets-costly-new-prison-paradigm/

5. **Wall Street Journal (2024)** – "$13 for a Video Call. $25 for a Movie. Tablets Connect Prisoners—at a Steep Price."
   - Not academic; journalism.

6. **NEED**: An academic peer-reviewed study specifically about inmate tablets. The Prison Policy Initiative reports are credible org reports. Slate survey is journalism.

## Subject area 2: parole-apps

### Strong candidates
1. **Owens et al. (2022)** – USENIX Security 2022: "Electronic Monitoring Smartphone Apps: An Analysis of Risks from Technical, Human-Centered, and Legal Perspectives."
   - Authors: Kentrell Owens, Anita Alem, Franziska Roesner, Tadayoshi Kohno
   - Venue: USENIX Security Symposium 2022
   - Method: Technical analysis (16 Android apps) + review analysis (257 user reviews) + privacy policy analysis
   - Key finding: apps had pervasive malfunctions (104/165 negative reviews mentioned malfunction); location failures; facial recognition failures; alerts at inappropriate times; risk of parole violation from app malfunction
   - DOI: Not stated on PDF; USENIX proceedings URL: https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity22/presentation/owens

2. **GWU report (2021)** – "Study Finds Issues with Electronic Ankle Monitors Used as Alternative to Incarceration" 
   - George Washington University report on ankle monitors
   - 101 agencies, 44 states, policy analysis
   - URL: https://gwtoday.gwu.edu/study-finds-issues-electronic-ankle-monitors-used-alternative-incarceration

3. **CJTEC Brief (2023)** – "Smartphone Applications for Community Supervision"
   - URL: https://globcci.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/CJTEC-Brief-Report-on-Smartphone-Applications-for-Community-Supervision.pdf
   - Desk-research brief on community supervision apps; foundational overview

4. **Wired (2020)** – "Apps Are Now Putting the Parole Agent in Your Pocket" – journalism, probably not suitable

5. **Need**: A study from the supervised person's perspective on UX of GPS/ankle monitor or check-in app.

## Subject area 3: BWC

### Confirmed candidates
1. **Lum et al. (2019)** – "Research on body-worn cameras: What we know, what we need to know." *Criminology & Public Policy*, 18(1), 93-118.
   - DOI: 10.1111/1745-9133.12412
   - Comprehensive narrative review of 70 studies; officer perceptions section covers how officers use cameras, chilling effects on discretion, etc.

2. **Jennings et al. (2014)** – "Officer perceptions of the use of body-worn cameras in law enforcement." *Journal of Criminal Justice*, 42(6), 549-556.
   - DOI: 10.1016/j.jcrimjus.2014.09.008
   - Survey of Orlando PD officers before BWC deployment; 62.7% favorable; perceived impact on behavior
   - Available: https://bja.ojp.gov/sites/g/files/xyckuh186/files/bwc/pdfs/usf_orlandopd_study.pdf

3. **Gaub et al. (2016)** – "Officer Perceptions of Body-Worn Cameras Before and After Deployment: A Study of Three Departments." *Police Quarterly*, 19(3), 275-302.
   - Authors: Janne E. Gaub, David E. Choate, Natalie Todak, Charles M. Katz, Michael D. White
   - Pre/post survey design in 3 western US departments
   - Available: https://cvpcs.asu.edu/sites/g/files/litvpz1111/files/content/pages/2016%20PQ%20Officer%20Attitudes%20BWCs.pdf

4. **Lum et al. (2020)** – "Body-worn cameras' effects on police officers and citizen behavior: A systematic review." *Campbell Systematic Reviews*, 16(3), e1112.
   - DOI: 10.1002/cl2.1112; PMC8356344
   - Meta-analysis of 30 studies; 16.6% reduction in citizen complaints

5. **Police Forum PERF (2014)** – "Implementing a Body-Worn Camera Program: Recommendations and Lessons Learned." Police Executive Research Forum.
   - Practice guide based on interviews with police chiefs; qualitative
   - URL: https://www.policeforum.org/assets/docs/Free_Online_Documents/Technology/implementing%20a%20body-worn%20camera%20program.pdf

## Subject area 4: predictive-policing

### Confirmed candidates
1. **Stevenson (2018)** – "Assessing Risk Assessment in Action." *Minnesota Law Review*, 103, 303.
   - URL: https://www.minnesotalawreview.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/01/13Stevenson_MLR.pdf
   - Analysis of Kentucky pretrial risk assessment in >1 million cases; shows judges only modestly followed tool recommendations
   - This is about how decision-makers actually USE the tool – strong UXR relevance

2. **Myhill & Hohl (2023)** – "The 'officer effect' in risk assessment for domestic abuse." *Policing and Society* (published in *European Journal of Criminology*).
   - DOI: 10.1177/14773708231156331
   - PMC10248280
   - Quantitative analysis of 3,079 DASH risk assessments + field observations; 17% of variance in scores explained by officer characteristics, not victim characteristics

3. **ShotSpotter Efficacy Study (2011/2016)** – commissioned study; vendor-produced; not ideal
   - Better: Topper working paper on ShotSpotter and unintended consequences (officer time allocation)
   
4. **Stevenson & Doleac (2018)** – "Roadblock to Reform" – ACS report analyzing pretrial risk assessment in Kentucky and sentencing risk assessment in Virginia
   - URL: https://www.acslaw.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/RoadblockToReformReport.pdf

5. **NACDL Task Force Report (2020)** – Risk assessment tool report
   - URL: https://www.nacdl.org/getattachment/a92d7c30-32d4-4b49-9c57-6c14ed0b9894/riskassessmentreportnovember182020.pdf

## Subject area 6: victim-notification

### Confirmed candidates
1. **Irazola et al. (2013/2015)** – "Keeping Victims Informed: Service Providers' and Victims' Experiences Using Automated Notification Systems." *Violence and Victims*, 30(3).
   - Survey: 1,246 service providers + 723 victims
   - Key finding: <23% of victims registered; challenges with registration and accuracy

2. **Zweig et al. (2013)** – "Automated Victim Notification: Awareness and Use Among Service Providers and Victims." NIJ Issue Brief, Sept 2013. NCJ 243840.
   - URL: https://bibliography.icpsr.umich.edu/bibliography/citations/data/179153/fileDownload
   - Same SAVIN evaluation, but an earlier issue brief

3. **CJTEC/NIJ (2024)** – "Automated Victim Notification Systems in the Criminal Justice System." October 2024. NCJ 310471.
   - URL: https://nij.ojp.gov/library/publications/automated-victim-notification-systems-criminal-justice-system
   - 12-page overview; identifies vendor landscape and implementation challenges; victim advocates say systems fail to meet victim needs

4. **ICPSR study 34593** – Irazola (2016) archival data: "Evaluation of the Statewide Automated Victim Information and Notification (SAVIN) Program [United States], 2010-2012"
   - DOI: 10.3886/ICPSR34593.v1
   - This is the underlying data for the NIJ SAVIN evaluation

## Subject area 7: tip-lines

### Confirmed candidates
1. **Hsieh & Heinze (2022)** – "The Effectiveness of the Say-Something Anonymous Reporting System in Preventing School Violence: A Cluster Randomized Control Trial in 19 Middle Schools." *Journal of School Violence*, 21(4), 413-428.
   - DOI: 10.1080/15388220.2022.2105858 (this Tandfonline URL from search)
   - NIJ-funded; cluster RCT; 19 middle schools in Miami; 13.5% fewer violent incidents

   - DOI: 10.1080/15388220.2021.1910518
   - Qualitative; parent and school personnel perspectives; implementation barriers

3. **Stein-Seroussi et al. (2024/2023)** – "Implementation of SafeVoice, Nevada Statewide Anonymous Tip Line for School-Age Youth."
   - Published in *Journal of School Violence*, 24(1), 154-167 (Jan 2025)
   - Also: NIJ Final Report, award 2016-CK-BX-0007, April 2024, NCJ 308905
   - Quantitative + qualitative; first 5 years of SafeVoice; 27,000+ tips; interrupted time-series; 32% reduction in violence against staff

## Subject area 9: jail-visitation

### Confirmed candidates
1. **Rabuy & Wagner (2015)** – "Screening Out Family Time: The for-profit video visitation industry in prisons and jails." Prison Policy Initiative, January 2015.
   - Authors: Bernadette Rabuy and Peter Wagner
   - Method: Contract analysis (388 jails), interviews, usage data, news review
   - Key finding: 74% of jails banned in-person visits when adopting video; high costs; technical failures
   - URL: https://www.prisonpolicy.org/visitation/ScreeningOutFamilyTime_January2015.pdf

## Notes on thin areas:
- **Tip-lines**: Good evidence for school safety tip-lines; almost nothing on Crime Stoppers UX from academic sources
- **Victim notification**: Limited UX studies; mainly system-level evaluation, not true usability research
- **Digital evidence tools**: Very limited UX/usability research; mostly workflow/operational studies
- **Inmate tablets**: Most research is institutional reports/journalism, not peer-reviewed; Open Campus survey is best available
- **Jail visitation**: PPI report is strong but institutional; no RCT or academic usability study exists for video visitation platforms per se
